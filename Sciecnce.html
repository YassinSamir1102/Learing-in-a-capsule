Physics
Friction Force
  1. Definition of Friction Force: Friction force is a resistive force that occurs when two surfaces interact, opposing the relative motion or attempted motion between them.

  2. Types of Friction:
    Static Friction: Prevents motion between two surfaces that are not moving relative to each other. It must be overcome to start moving.
    Kinetic (Sliding) Friction: Acts on objects that are sliding over a surface. Rolling Friction: Occurs when an object rolls over a surface.
    Fluid Friction (Drag): Resistance by fluids (liquids or gases) against an object's motion through the fluid.

  3. Factors Affecting Friction:
    Surface Roughness: Rougher surfaces generally have higher friction.
    Normal Force: The perpendicular force exerted by a surface on an object; greater normal force increases friction.
    Material Properties: Different materials have different frictional properties (coefficients of friction).
    Presence of Lubricants: Lubricants reduce friction by creating a thin layer between surfaces.

Gravitational Force
  1. Definition: Gravitational force is the attractive force between two masses. It is one of the four fundamental forces of nature.

  2. Characteristics of Gravitational Force
    Universal: Acts between all masses, regardless of their nature or state.
    Attractive: Always pulls masses together, never pushes them apart.
    Inverse Square Law: The force decreases with the square of the distance between the masses. If the distance is doubled, the force is reduced by a factor of four.
    Vector Quantity: Has both magnitude and direction. The direction is along the line joining the centers of two masses.


  3. Effects of Gravitational Force:
    Weight: The gravitational force exerted by Earth on an object gives the object its weight.
    Orbital Motion: Keeps planets in orbit around the Sun, moons in orbit around planets, and governs the motion of satellites.
    Tides: The gravitational pull of the Moon and the Sun causes the rise and fall of sea levels on Earth, known as tides.


  4. Einstein's Theory of General Relativity:
    Curvature of Spacetime: Proposes that gravity is not a force but a consequence of the curvature of spacetime caused by the uneven distribution of mass.
    Geodesics: Objects move along the paths (geodesics) in curved spacetime. Massive objects cause more curvature, affecting the motion of other objects.
    Gravitational Waves: Ripples in spacetime caused by certain movements of mass, such as the collision of black holes. Predicted by Einstein and confirmed by observations.
Biology
Cell Biology Cells: The fundamental unit of life. All living organisms are made up of cells, which can be either prokaryotic (lacking a nucleus) or eukaryotic (having a nucleus).
Genetics DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid): The molecule that carries genetic information. It is composed of two strands forming a double helix, and it encodes the instructions for the development, functioning, growth, and reproduction of all living organisms.
Evolution Natural Selection: The process by which organisms better adapted to their environment tend to survive and produce more offspring. This is a key mechanism of evolution.
Ecology Ecosystems: Communities of living organisms interacting with their physical environment. Ecology studies these interactions and the relationships between organisms and their habitats.
Physiology Organ Systems: Groups of organs that work together to perform specific functions (e.g., the digestive system, respiratory system). Physiology examines how these systems function and maintain life.
